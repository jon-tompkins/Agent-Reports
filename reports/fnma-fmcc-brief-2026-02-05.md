## WTF Is Going On?

Fannie Mae (FNMA) and Freddie Mac (FMCC) have been in government conservatorship since **September 2008**. That's 17+ years. These two companies back roughly **$7 trillion** in mortgages‚Äînearly half of all US home loans.

The stocks trade on OTC markets (not NYSE/NASDAQ) at a fraction of their book value. Common shares have been in limbo, waiting for an "exit" that keeps getting promised but never delivered.

## The Bull Case üêÇ

**"This time it's different"** ‚Äî yeah, I know. But here's why some believe it:

1. **Political tailwinds**: The current administration has signaled interest in housing finance reform. Treasury has more flexibility now than in previous years.

2. **Massive book value**: Combined book value is ~$140B+. Common shares trade at ~$3-6. If they exit conservatorship with shareholder value intact, the upside is potentially 5-20x.

3. **Profitable machines**: Post-2012, both companies have been wildly profitable. They've sent ~$300B to Treasury. The "bailout" has been repaid many times over.

4. **Legal precedent**: Courts have ruled the Net Worth Sweep (where Treasury took 100% of profits) was legal, but political pressure could still force a resolution.

5. **Capital building**: They've been retaining earnings since 2019. Getting closer to required capital levels every quarter.

---

## The Bear Case üêª

**"This has been 'about to happen' for 17 years":**

1. **Political football**: Housing finance reform requires Congress OR creative Treasury action. Both are hard. Neither party wants to "own" any changes that could affect housing prices.

2. **The Senior Preferred Problem**: Treasury holds $190B+ in senior preferred stock with liquidation preference. Common shareholders are junior to this. Any exit plan needs to address this‚Äîlikely through conversion, cancellation, or massive dilution.

3. **Dilution risk**: The most likely exit scenarios involve issuing billions in new shares. Your $5 share could easily become $1 post-offering.

4. **OTC hellscape**: These trade on pink sheets. Liquidity sucks. Spreads are wide. Getting in is easy; getting out at your price isn't.

5. **Legal uncertainty**: Shareholders have sued. They've mostly lost. Courts have upheld the government's broad powers during conservatorship.

## Major Risks ‚ö†Ô∏è

1. **Total loss possible**: Common shares could go to zero. This isn't FUD‚Äîit's a real scenario if exit involves wiping equity.

2. **Political 180**: One tweet, one policy shift, one new Treasury Secretary could tank the thesis.

3. **Decades more waiting**: There's no legal requirement to ever exit conservatorship. They could stay in zombie mode indefinitely.

4. **Dilution to oblivion**: Even a "successful" exit could mean your shares get diluted 90%+.

5. **Liquidity trap**: When news hits, spreads blow out. You might not exit at your target price.
